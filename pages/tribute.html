In Memoriam

Those Who Folded First

"A single sheet of paper, a thousand possibilities."

"They gave us more than a group. They gave us a reason to gather, a language made of folds, and a community that outlived them both." — The Members of GSDOG
◈Photo of Florence

Florence Temko

Co-Founder · Author · Teacher

Florence Temko was one of the most prolific and beloved authors in the world of origami and paper crafts. Over the course of her career she wrote more than 60 books — works that introduced origami to generations of children and adults who had never before thought to pick up a square of paper.

Her talent was translation: she could take the intimidating geometry of a complex fold and render it warm, clear, and inviting. She understood that origami wasn't just an art form — it was a way of connecting people, of giving hands something meaningful to do, of turning a quiet afternoon into something worth remembering.

Florence co-founded the Greater San Diego Origami Group in the early 1990s, bringing her spirit of generosity and accessibility to the local community. Her meetings were welcoming to beginners and experts alike, and she modeled a kind of enthusiasm that proved infectious.

We miss her. We fold in her name.

V'Ann Cornelius

Co-Founder · Curator · Innovator

V'Ann Cornelius saw origami as what it has always truly been: art. Her vision helped bring paper folding out of living rooms and into the halls of museums, where it deserved to be seen.

She co-curated Origami Masterworks, one of the first major museum-based origami exhibitions anywhere in the world, held at the Mingei International Museum in Balboa Park. The exhibition broke new ground not only in presenting origami as a serious fine art form, but in how that work was displayed — V'Ann's innovations in exhibition design gave the models the dignity and context they deserved.

Her reputation extended far beyond San Diego. She was known internationally for her contributions to origami's cultural standing, and her work helped change how the art world understood paper folding.

She co-founded this group with Florence, sharing her conviction that origami could be both high art and a communal joy. Both turned out to be true. Both always were.
